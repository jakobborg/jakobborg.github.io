A personal philosophy

On Being Finite
and Open

Notes toward a self-derived way of living

Finitude

There are roughly 4,000 weeks in a human life. The to-do list will never be empty. No system, no discipline, no optimization will ever produce a state of done. This is not a problem to be solved. It is the actual condition of being alive.

The anxious feeling that you should be doing more is not information about your performance. It is the feeling of being finite — which no amount of productivity can fix. Accepting limits is not defeatism. It is the only honest starting point.

You have roughly 4,000 weeks. The question is not how to do everything. The question is what to do with what you actually have. After Oliver Burkeman, Four Thousand Weeks

Residue & Resonance

Not all time feels the same. Some activities are acoustically alive — the world reaches toward you and you reach back, and in that moment of live contact something amplifies. You are more present than you were. The experience vibrates at a frequency your whole being recognizes. This is resonance. It is not something left behind. It is what happens in the moment itself.

Resonance is the quality of genuine encounter — with the forest, with the Lyra-8 at midnight, with a dog lying in a hole you dug with your own hands, with music that finds something in you ordinary days cannot reach. It cannot be forced or manufactured. It requires only that you be present enough to be reached.

Resonance leaves an echo. As in acoustics — a resonant space rings long after the moment of vibration. The fullness you carry through the rest of the day after a real walk, the quiet satisfaction of finished physical work, the way a concert can inhabit you for days afterward — these are echoes. Evidence that something real occurred. A long echo tells you the room was alive. No echo at all tells you it was dead — that you were technically present but nothing vibrated.

Residue is different entirely. It is what lingers from avoidance — the skipped walk, the drifted afternoon, the open loop of an unfinished thing. Not an echo of something real, but the low-grade hum of something that never happened. It does not point anywhere useful. It simply accumulates.

A practical test: if I skip this, will I feel its absence later today? That absence is the residue of a resonant moment that did not occur. The echo that never had a chance to sound.

In acoustic terms, every finite life has a noise floor — the permanent background hum of being a conscious human with more undone than done, more unlived than lived. This cannot be eliminated. Finitude itself generates it. But residue raises the noise floor further. Each avoided thing, each drifted afternoon, each open loop adds interference. Enough of it, and the noise swallows the signal entirely. The insistence is still sounding. The world is still reaching. But you can no longer isolate it from the background.

The practice, then, is not silence. It is signal training — learning to orient toward resonance even with the noise present. Closing loops lowers the residue layer. The containers and anchors keep returning you to the signal. Not a quiet life, but a life where the noise floor is low enough that the ordinary resonance of a walk, a meal, an evening at the Octatrack, can still find you.

The Drift

The drift is what happens between real choices. It feels like keeping options open. It is actually a refusal to accept the discomfort of choosing — because choosing means accepting that the unchosen things are not happening today, and that is the feeling the drift is designed to avoid.

Passive avoidance

The task hovers. You are not doing it, but not free of it. Neither working nor resting. Suspended between the two, paying a continuous tax on your attention.

Active neglect

A conscious decision: this is not happening today. The task still exists. But the loop closes mentally. You are free to be where you are.

The phone is the perfect drift machine. Infinite possibility, infinite novelty, no repetition, no confrontation with the fact that life is largely made of the same things done again and again. It does not give resonance. It suspends you in the absence of it.

Lazy

Horizontal on the couch knowing that is exactly where you should be.

Lost

Horizontal on the couch wondering where the day went.

Same couch. Only one leaves residue.

Anxiety & Freedom

Anxiety is not the obstacle to a real encounter. It is the proof that one is possible. You are anxious precisely because you are free — because the outcome is genuinely open, because something real is at stake, because you have not yet decided who you will be in this moment. The discomfort is the feeling of standing at a genuine threshold.

Before the hardest conversations — the ones that mattered most, with the person who mattered most — there was a choice. Prepare a script. Manage the exposure. Arrive protected, controlled, present in body but safely distant in every way that counts. This is what avoidance looks like when it is dressed as responsibility.

The other choice: do not prepare. Sit with the anxiety instead of converting it into a plan. Let it be uncomfortable. Let it be unresolved. And then speak from inside it — unscripted, unprotected, genuinely there. Not performing honesty but being it.

Through anxiety you experience the freedom to choose. The anxiety is not what stands between you and the real conversation. It is the doorway into it. After Søren Kierkegaard, The Concept of Anxiety

The prepared script is a form of drift — a way of being technically present while remaining elsewhere. What Rosa calls resonance cannot occur from behind it. You cannot genuinely reach another person from a protected position, and they cannot reach you. The unscripted conversation, entered with full exposure, is one of the places resonance most readily happens — precisely because the stakes are real and nothing is controlled.

Knowing your own avoidant tendencies and choosing differently anyway, in the moments that count most — that is not a small thing. Kierkegaard would call it a leap. Not into certainty, but into the genuine uncertainty of being fully present with another person, with no script to fall back on and no guaranteed outcome. Just the anxiety, and through it, the freedom.

Facing Finitude

Procrastination is not laziness. It is identity protection. As long as you have not fully tried, the possibility remains open — you might still be the brilliant architect, the great maker of things. The moment you commit and do the work, you find out what you actually are: a real person with real limits, some genuinely good, some not what you hoped.

The question is which pain is more bearable: the finitude you face by trying, or the slow bleed of never finding out. Most people choose the slow bleed because it is diffuse and deniable. But it has its own residue. A heavy one.

Ordinary daily tasks carry a different avoidance. Not am I good enough but is this really what my finite time looks like — a low-grade protest against the repetitive texture of being alive. The phone offers an exit from this. But the ordinary tasks, done with presence rather than protest, are where much of the resonance actually lives.

The Insistence

The world reaches toward you. Not with force — with insistence. A call that does not compel but persists. The forest on a slow morning. The quality of light through a Japanese maple. Music that arrives at something in you that ordinary days cannot reach. A dog that simply needs the walk.

You can be available to this or not. The drift closes the aperture. The phone closes it. Passive avoidance closes it. What keeps it open are the containers — the recurring structures that bring you back into contact with what is real.

The experience is the same whether you call it God or resonance. What differs is the interpretive framework. The aperture is the same aperture. After John D. Caputo, The Insistence of God

A Christian and an agnostic can stand in the same forest and feel the same wholeness. What differs is what they call it afterward. The openness itself — the willingness to be reached — is what matters. And that openness requires tending, because the drift is always available and the insistence never forces its way in.

Containers as Anchors

Without structure, the openness dissolves. This is the structural weakness of a self-derived philosophy — there is no institution maintaining it, no community noticing your absence, no Sunday that insists. You have to keep rediscovering it.

Containers are pre-made decisions that do not need to be remade each time. They reduce the number of in-the-moment choices where the drift lives. But more than that — the right containers are anchors. Recurring collisions with what is real, what resonates, what keeps you available to the insistence.

The anchors are not arbitrary. They are identified through the residue test — the things that leave resonance when you do them and residue when you skip them. You already know what they are.

  • The walk — daily, with Senja, non-negotiable. She does not defer.
  • The forest — slower, wider, the wholesomeness that arrives without being summoned.
  • Physical work with a real outcome — the hole, the garden, the wall.
  • Cooking well, for no occasion, because Tuesday evening is a real occasion.
  • The Lab — the space that signals what happens there.
  • Composing — hardware, friction, the gap between vision and execution, showing up anyway.
  • Music that reaches something in you that ordinary days cannot.

These are not a productivity system. They are a life arranged to keep bumping you back toward openness.

Doing for the Sake of Being

Instrumental thinking hollows everything out eventually. If the walk is just exercise, if cooking is just nutrition, if the festival was just entertainment — they become means to ends and lose the quality that made them worth doing.

Some things are complete in themselves. Roy Khan singing The Black Halo front to back is not a stepping stone to anything. The hole with a dog lying in it is not useful. The bark under the maple does not need to be beautiful — but you need it to be. These are not indulgences. They are the thing itself.

Modern acceleration does not destroy meaning directly. It destroys the capacity to be touched by things. Resonance requires stillness that acceleration makes impossible. After Hartmut Rosa, Resonance

The question is not whether your leisure is improving you. The question is whether you were present for it — whether something reached you and you reached back.

Friction & the Making of Things

There is a gap between what you hear in your head and what your hands can currently produce. This gap is not a problem. It is the engine. A practice without that tension — where execution perfectly matches vision — would hollow out and stop mattering. The discrepancy is what keeps you showing up.

Composing on hardware — the Digitakt, the Digitone, the Octatrack, the Lyra-8 — is a choice to work with instruments that push back. They have opinions. The Octatrack will humble anyone. The Lyra-8 is not programmed so much as coaxed; it is almost alive, and what emerges from the negotiation is often more interesting than what was intended. You could do more, faster, on a laptop with unlimited options. But more and faster is not the point.

Self-imposed limitation is not deprivation. It is the condition that makes expression possible. Infinite freedom produces nothing. The frame — the constraint of a specific instrument, a specific key, a specific set of sounds that resist you — is what forces something real to emerge. The limitation and the meaning are not in spite of each other. They are the same thing.

You show up anyway. You try because it is meaningful, not because you are good at it yet. That is the only honest relationship with a practice.

AI-produced music removes the friction entirely. A perfect composition, generated without struggle, without the gap, without the negotiation between intention and limitation. It is resonance-proof. Nothing reached through it, nothing reached back. Not because the output is necessarily ugly — but because no one was changed in the making of it. The practice of composing is not primarily about the composition. It is about who you become in the hours of trying.

You compose not for the world but for yourself. No audience to satisfy, no validation to seek. Late evening, hardware in front of you, finding out what emerges when you show up despite the gap. This is doing for the sake of being in its purest form — complete in itself, justified by nothing outside it.

One Open Loop at a Time

The hole first. Then the maple. Then the patio wall. Each one closed before the next opens. Not because efficiency demands it, but because this is how things actually get finished — and finishing things is one of the ways the world feels real and inhabitable rather than weightless and overwhelming.

The drift keeps everything in progress and nothing complete. Serializing — one real thing, then the next — produces the experience of a life actually lived rather than endlessly managed.

Small experiments, honestly evaluated. Try the thing. Check what it leaves behind. You do not need to decide in advance whether it will matter. The residue and resonance will tell you.

The Checklist

A philosophy built on rejecting productivity systems, productivity-systemised. The irony is the point.

  • You have 4,000 weeks. The list will never be empty. Stop waiting for done.
  • Notice the drift. Name it. Convert it into a real choice — either work or rest, never both and neither.
  • Ask the residue question: if I skip this, will I feel its absence tonight? The answer is your actual priority list.
  • Walk with Senja. Every day. Non-negotiable. She does not care about your to-do list.
  • Distinguish lazy from lost. Lazy is chosen. Lost is what happens when you don't choose.
  • Close one loop before opening the next. The hole, then the maple, then the wall. In that order.
  • When the anxiety arrives before something that matters — do not prepare it away. Sit in it. It is the doorway, not the obstacle. Through anxiety you experience the freedom to choose.
  • Put the phone down. It is a resonance-free zone dressed as possibility.
  • Cook a real meal on a random Tuesday. You are worth the occasion.
  • Show up to the Octatrack even when — especially when — the gap between what you hear and what you can make feels unbearable. The gap is the engine.
  • Do not optimise your leisure. A power metal festival does not need to make you more productive.
  • Keep the aperture open. The insistence is always there. You just have to stay available to it.
  • Build containers. Not as a productivity system — as anchors. Structures that keep bumping you back toward what is real.
  • Check the echo. A long echo means something real happened. No echo means you were in a dead room, even if you were technically present.
  • Do not mistake the checklist for the philosophy. Close this document. Go outside.
